Wait, `[insert subject]` is complex, actually?
Yes, as are most things.

## What do I mean?

I dislike dismissing things that are complex as simple.
For one, it diminishes the work that many thousands of individuals have performed; for another, this notion very often obscures details that are actually essential in forming an understanding of a topic.
As a graduate student, I've seen this sentiment arise from professionals and professors alike, and every time I notice it, I'm irked.
You may think that insisting on complexity is a form of "gatekeeping," thus dissuading new folks from beginning the learning process, but I would like to argue that the opposite is true.
It wouldn't surprise me if this phenomenon is more common in the realm of STEM, considering the various types of ego that unfortunately tend to develop in these fields, but I believe my argument is subject-agnostic and may apply to nearly anything in life.

Keep in mind that I'm using the word "complex" here, not "complicated," because it carries a more positive connotation, generally speaking.
My general thesis is such: acknowledging that things are complex and accepting reality is more constructive and beneficial than oversimplifying and purposely or inadvertently burying the lede.

Examples:
- "GNU/Linux is a better name than Linux." Here, the offender wants to act high and mighty for knowing more than thou, when in fact they're also wrong.
- "Oversimplification of thing I think I understand, but actually don't, is really easy! I don't get why you're spending so much time on this." I don't know if this would be considered akin to the Dunning--Kruger effect, but it's extremely annoying.
